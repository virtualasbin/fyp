1. Problem definition 
 
1.1. Aim  
 
This project aims to create a simple, reliable web app that helps university students organise their 
coursework and deadlines in one place while demonstrating strong, full-lifecycle software 
engineering practice. 
 
1.2. Problem description 
 
Time management remains one of the most significant contributors to low academic performance. 
Most students begin the term with plenty of motivation, but that sense of control quickly fades once 
multiple deadlines collide and priorities shift unexpectedly. In reality, a student might receive 
assignment briefs through one platform, feedback through another, and timetable changes via email. 
This scattered flow of information makes planning far more complicated than it needs to be. The 
outcome is a familiar cycle: work begins later than intended, the quality drops as submission dates 
approach, stress rises, and confidence gradually declines (Macan, 1990). 
Many of the tools students currently rely on are either too generic or too demanding for academic 
use. Paper planners are straightforward but easy to misplace and inconvenient to update. Basic 
to-do list apps are simple and accessible, yet they often fail to support academic specific needs such 
as grouping tasks by module, assigning weighted priorities, or setting reminders based on deadlines. 
On the opposite end of the spectrum, advanced productivity platforms can feel overwhelming for 
students who only want a focused study planner. Some of these platforms also hide essential 
features behind paid subscriptions, creating an affordability barrier that discourages long-term use. 
A further challenge is the inconsistency of access across different devices. Students frequently 
switch between smartphones, university lab computers, and personal laptops throughout the day. 
When planning data is not synchronised or easily accessible across all these contexts, users lose 
trust in the tool and revert to improvised notes or scattered reminders. This project aims to close 
that gap by developing a web based system with a responsive design and lightweight interaction 
patterns. The intention is to support real daily behaviour: quickly checking what needs to be done 
today, adding a new task in seconds, and receiving reminders before urgency turns into panic. 
 
1.3. Scope 
 
The scope of this project sets clear boundaries around what will and will not be delivered during 
this academic work. Defining these limits early helps maintain focus and ensures that the final 
outcome is both realistic and achievable within the module’s timeframe. 
 
In scope are the essential features needed to create a functional and student-centred task 
management system. These include user registration and login, full task CRUD operations, and 
support for categories and priority tags. The system will also handle deadline and status tracking, 
reminder notifications, dashboard summaries, and filtering and sorting options. A responsive 
interface will be developed to ensure smooth use on both mobile and desktop browsers. In addition, 
the project will incorporate basic analytics such as overdue task counts, completion rates, and 
upcoming deadlines along with secure data storage using a relational model and validation to 
prevent common user input errors. 
 
Out of scope are features that, while valuable in professional productivity tools, exceed the time 
and complexity constraints of this module. These include fully developed native mobile 
applications, enterprise-level collaboration workflows, institution-wide integration with learning 
management systems, and advanced machine-learning-based scheduling recommendations.

Although these capabilities could enhance the system in the future, excluding them now helps 
maintain a manageable workload and keeps attention on the core needs of students. 
The scope also covers documentation and evaluation. This means the final submission is not just a 
working application but a complete engineering process supported by design reasoning, testing 
evidence, and reflection on limitations. By defining the scope early, the project avoids feature creep 
and supports structured, realistic planning throughout development. 
 
 
1.4. Intended outcome 
 
The intended outcome of this project is to deliver a reliable and genuinely usable application that 
enables students to plan their work with less friction and fewer missed deadlines. The final system 
should allow users to create tasks quickly, attach meaningful context such as module, due date, and 
priority, and monitor their progress through simple and intuitive visual summaries. The interface 
must remain clear and manageable even under real academic workload conditions, particularly 
during busy weeks when many tasks are active at the same time. 
From an academic perspective, the intended outcome also involves demonstrating that software 
development decisions were made deliberately and supported by clear reasoning. These decisions 
should be tested against criteria that reflect real user needs and behaviours. A successful result will 
show competence in key areas such as web development, database modelling, authentication 
mechanisms, and front-end usability design. It will also provide evidence that project management 
choices were realistic and that potential risks were addressed thoughtfully rather than overlooked.

1.5. Objectives 
 
To achieve the overall aim of the project, a set of clear and structured objectives has been defined. 
These objectives guide both the development process and the final functionality of the system, 
ensuring that the application meets the academic and practical needs of its intended users. 
 
Primary Objectives 
 
The first set of objectives focuses on the core functionality required to deliver a meaningful and 
effective task-management solution for students. 
 
• To design and develop a Progressive Web Application tailored to students’ academic needs. 
The system should operate seamlessly across devices and provide a consistent experience whether 
accessed on a laptop, tablet, or smartphone. As a Progressive Web Application, it must combine the 
accessibility of a website with the reliability and convenience of an installed app, ensuring students 
can manage their workload anytime and anywhere. 
• To implement secure user authentication and data management. Protecting user information 
is essential, especially when dealing with personal schedules and academic commitments. The 
project aims to incorporate secure login mechanisms, proper password handling, and structured data 
management practices to maintain privacy and trust. 
• To enable efficient task creation, tracking, and prioritisation. Students should be able to add 
tasks quickly, organise them by module or category, and assign priorities that reflect their academic 
workload. The system must support smooth tracking of progress and provide clear visibility of what 
needs attention. 
• To integrate push notifications and smart reminders. Timely reminders are crucial for 
preventing missed deadlines. The application will include push notifications and intelligent 

 3 
reminder logic to alert students before tasks become urgent, helping them stay ahead of their 
workload. 
• To provide offline functionality using service workers. Recognising that students may not 
always have stable internet access, the system will support offline use. Service workers will allow 
essential features such as viewing tasks or adding new ones to function even without connectivity, 
improving reliability in real-world scenarios. 
 
Secondary Objectives 
 
The secondary objectives support the broader quality, usability, and maintainability of the system. 
While not the core functional requirements, they significantly enhance the overall value of the 
application. 
 
• To design a responsive and accessible user interface based on Human-Computer Interaction 
(HCI) principles. The interface should be intuitive, visually clear, and accessible to a wide range of 
users. Applying HCI principles ensures that the design supports efficient interaction, reduces 
cognitive load, and accommodates different devices and screen sizes. 
• To develop a relational database for secure and structured data storage. A well-designed 
relational database will ensure that user data, tasks, categories, and reminders are stored securely 
and efficiently. This structure supports data integrity, scalability, and reliable performance. 
• To incorporate productivity analytics and performance dashboards. Students benefit from 
insights into their study habits. The system will include analytics such as completion rates, overdue 
task counts, and upcoming deadlines, presented through clear dashboard components that help users 
understand their progress. 
• To conduct system testing, usability evaluation, and performance analysis. Thorough testing 
is essential to ensure the system behaves reliably under real conditions. This includes functional 
testing, usability assessments, and performance checks to identify issues early and refine the user 
experience. 
• To deploy the application using modern web hosting platforms. Deployment ensures that the 
system is accessible to users in a stable and scalable environment. Using contemporary hosting 
solutions supports reliability, security, and ease of maintenance. 
 
• To produce comprehensive technical documentation and reports. Clear documentation is vital 
for demonstrating the engineering process behind the system. This includes design rationale, 
implementation details, testing evidence, and reflective analysis, ensuring the project is 
academically robust and professionally presented. 
 
 
 
2. Contextual research 
 
2.1. Context investigation 
 
Effective time management is a critical determinant of academic success. University students must 
balance coursework, assignments, examinations, part-time employment, and personal commitments. 
Without proper planning and organisation, students often experience stress, reduced productivity, 
and missed deadlines. In today's fast-paced educational environment, digital tools have become 
essential for managing academic responsibilities efficiently. 
The rapid advancement of information technology has transformed traditional approaches to 
organisation and productivity. Students increasingly rely on smartphones, tablets, and laptops for 
learning and communication (Trueman, 1996). As a result, there is a growing demand for digital 

 4 
solutions that provide accessibility, flexibility, and efficiency across multiple platforms. Task 
management applications have emerged as essential tools to support students in organising their 
academic and personal schedules. 
Despite the availability of various productivity tools, many existing solutions are not tailored 
specifically to academic needs. Some applications are overly complex, require subscription fees, or 
lack features such as deadline prioritisation, offline functionality, and performance analytics. 
Additionally, students frequently switch between devices, creating a need for cross-platform 
compatibility and seamless data synchronisation. 
 
Progressive Web Applications (PWAs) provide an innovative solution by combining the advantages 
of web and mobile applications (Developers, 2023). PWAs are accessible through web browsers 
while offering app-like features such as offline functionality, installability, push notifications, and 
responsive interfaces (Network, 2024). These characteristics make PWAs particularly suitable for 
students who require reliable and accessible tools to manage their academic workload. 
The proposed Student Task Management Web Application aims to address these challenges by 
delivering a cost-effective, user-friendly, and installable system. By leveraging modern web 
technologies, the application will support students in organising tasks, tracking deadlines, and 
enhancing productivity, thereby contributing to improved academic performance and reduced 
stress.

3.3. Design (including system design, data design, and User experience design) 
 
• System design for the proposed application follows a standard and well-structured web 
architecture, consisting of three core layers: the presentation layer, the application layer, and 
the data layer. Each layer has a distinct responsibility, and this separation of concerns makes 
the system easier to test, maintain, and extend over time (Developers, 2023). The 
presentation layer manages the user interface, handling all interaction and visual display 
elements. The application layer contains the business logic, where server endpoints process 
validation, enforce rules, and manage data operations. Finally, the data layer is responsible 
for persistence, ensuring that all user and task information is stored securely and retrieved 
efficiently when needed.  

 8 
 
  
                                       Figure: System Architecture Diagram 
 
 
 
 
• Data design is based on a relational structure centred around the user and task tables, with 
optional fields for categories and reminders. This approach supports a clear one-to-many 
relationship, where each user can own multiple tasks. The relational model ensures data 
integrity and allows the system to scale as users create more tasks. To improve performance, 
especially for dashboard views, key fields such as due dates and task statuses are indexed. 
This optimisation ensures that queries remain fast even as the dataset grows, allowing the 
system to deliver timely and accurate information to the user.

tasks quickly, seeing priorities clearly, and making updates feel effortless. These aren’t

someone is juggling lectures, deadlines, part-time jobs, and society commitments, they don’t 
have time to fight with a complicated interface. They need something that responds instantly

The dashboard reflects this reality. It brings the most time-sensitive information today’s tasks and

Everything else is still accessible, but it doesn’t crowd the screen or distract from the essentials.

4. Planning 
 
4.1. Methodology 
An iterative, Agile-inspired approach is used for this project because requirements naturally become 
clearer once early prototypes are tested and feedback is gathered. Instead of attempting to finalise 
every detail upfront, the work is divided into short, manageable cycles. Each cycle follows a simple 
rhythm: define the goals, build a small functional increment, test it, collect feedback, and adjust. 
This approach ensures that progress continues even when initial assumptions change, and it allows 
the system to evolve in response to real user needs rather than theoretical expectations. 
The methodology places strong emphasis on producing tangible deliverables rather than relying 
solely on documentation to demonstrate progress. At the end of each cycle, there should be a 
verifiable output such as a working login system, functional task CRUD operations, a reminder 
workflow, or dashboard metrics. These concrete outputs provide objective evidence of momentum 
and make it easier to identify risks early. When something is not working as expected, it becomes 
visible sooner, allowing corrective action before issues escalate. 
Testing is integrated into every cycle rather than postponed until the end of development. This 
continuous testing approach includes checking form validation, verifying the authentication flow, 
ensuring task state transitions behave correctly, and confirming that the responsive layout adapts 
properly across devices. When defects appear repeatedly, the process includes a root-cause analysis 
to understand why the issue persists and to guide improvements in both code quality and 
development practices. 
Review checkpoints are designed to evaluate the system from both technical and user-oriented 
perspectives. Technical reviews focus on code behaviour, data integrity, and system stability, 
ensuring that the underlying architecture functions correctly. User-focused reviews, on the other 
hand, assess how quickly tasks can be completed, how intuitive the interface feels, and whether the 
design supports efficient daily use. Combining these two perspectives prevents the common mistake 
of declaring a feature “complete” simply because it compiles or passes a narrow functional test. 
Instead, a feature is only considered complete when it works reliably and delivers a smooth, 
meaningful experience for the user. 
 
4.2. Initial plans 
 
Initial planning for the project was organised across a series of structured phases: requirements and 
research, architecture and UI design, core implementation, testing and refinement, and final 
documentation. Each phase was aligned with the module’s assessment checkpoints to avoid 
last-minute pressure and ensure steady, manageable progress throughout the semester. This phased 
approach provided a clear roadmap and helped maintain focus on both technical development and 
academic deliverables.

Phase 1 concentrated on clarifying user needs and translating them into measurable, actionable 
requirements. This involved analysing student workflows, identifying pain points, and defining the 
essential features the system needed to support. 
 
 Phase 2 focused on producing early design artefacts, including wireframes, draft data models, and 
decisions about the technology stack. These outputs served as the foundation for implementation 
and ensured that development began with a clear vision. 
 
Phase 3 marked the start of core implementation. The priority was to build secure authentication 
and basic task operations, as these form the backbone of the system. Once these were stable, 
development moved on to more advanced features such as reminder functionality and dashboard 
components.  
 
Phase 4 emphasised bug fixing, usability improvements, and gathering evaluation evidence to 
demonstrate that the system met its intended objectives. 
Contingency time was deliberately built into the plan because student projects often encounter 
unexpected delays, whether from overlapping coursework deadlines or technical challenges. Buffer 
time was reserved for integration issues, design adjustments, and improving the quality of 
documentation. This flexibility helped maintain the standard of the final report without 
compromising the completeness of core features. 
The initial plan also included a final hardening window dedicated to regression testing and aligning 
all documentation. This stage is particularly important because late-stage changes can 
unintentionally break previously stable features. By reserving time for thorough retesting, the 
project reduced the risk of last-minute failures and increased confidence in the system’s stability 
during demonstration. 
Communication planning was simple but intentional. Weekly progress notes, issue tracking, and 
decision logs were maintained throughout development. These records supported reflective writing 
and made it easier to justify why certain features were reduced, delayed, or redesigned. They also 
provided a transparent trail of reasoning that strengthened the academic credibility of the project.
